A note about general academic writing

  1. Put your main point up front. Writing is different to speaking, and the reader tends to lose focus if we give a whole bunch of background information first and then build our argument with all the relevant details and then make our point at the end (see, didn't you lose the plot a little bit when you had to read that?). 

  2. Stick to ONE point per paragraph, and make that point CLOSE TO THE START of the paragraph. You may need to break up some of the longer paragraphs if you think there's more than one point in there. 

  3. Use linking words/phrases at the start of a new paragraph (“however”, “additionally”, “in contrast”, “therefore”). This will help the reader to follow your argument flow.  Make sure you don't overdo it though – i.e. not on every single paragraph.

  4. Vary your sentence length. Using a shorter sentence is an effective way of bringing the reader's attention to something. Check the longer sentences (anything that goes over 3-4 lines of type) and see if you can clarify them.

  5. Maintain a “golden thread” (= main point/central theme) through your writing. This thread should be there in the abstract, the first paragraph, and followed throughout your writing. Especially ensure it's in the conclusion/summary.

  6. Read your writing out aloud. This will help you to get a sense of the rhythm and flow of your sentences, and will help spot any typos or areas that need more linking. You might feel silly doing it, but you’ll pick up mistakes that you otherwise don’t notice.

  7. The Nature Masterclass on writing and publishing presentation is available for download. There is lots of useful information in this presentation, but you may wish to focus on:

    • Day 1, Session 3: Elements of Writing Style (p.54 - 72)

    • Day 1, Session 4: Titles and Abstracts (p.74 - 92)

    • Day 1, Session 5: Introduction to Conclusion (p.98 - 114)

    • Day 2, Session 2: Writing a Research Summary (p. 197 - 211)
