Introduction

In order to assess diverse impacts on marine life, data from relevant online databases is retrieved. Of these, the World Register of Marine Species (WoRMS) and the Ocean Biogeographic Information System (OBIS) are most relevant as they give basic information on species' taxonomies and their occurrences, the latter as coordinates. This basic information might be complemented with specific data from the Encyclopedia of Life (EoL) for each species.
For now, the focus lies on developing a characterization factor for marine invasive species within the Life Cycle Assessment framework. Hence, impacts and introduction pathways of marine invasive species are examined. Doing so, other relevant databases are used additionally which are: the Global Invasive Species Database (GISD), the Invasive Species Compendium (CABI), and the Nature Conservancy's global review.

Databases

DatabasesRelevant information

WoRMS

WorMS provides information on species' taxonomies and, if available, on their native and alien distribution (country-wise or as sea areas), introduction pathways, and impacts. Content and consistency of the latter categories are, however, limited.

OBIS

This database is linked to WoRMS, thus retrieves taxonomic information from it and appends distribution records and data on environmental conditions.

EoL

The Encyclopedia of Life provides, among others, physical descriptions and life histories of species.

GISD

Both the Global Invasive Species Database and the Invasive Species Compendium provide information on, among others, marine invasive species which is rather detailed. In particular, information on species' introductions and impacts are more comprehensive than in other databases. However, not all invasive species that are marked by WoRMS as such are covered in these.

CABI

The Nature Conservancy

This global review of marine invasive species conducted by Molnar et al. (2008) is provided in form of a downloadable MS Access database. In addition to species accounts, threat scores are assigned. As sources served various databases, including some of the other here mentioned, and a multitude of journal articles and other formats. However, this database is not updated any longer and only gives a status quo.

Marine Regions

This website contains various maps of marine regions according to different classifications. Based on a publication by Spalding et al. (2007), sea areas can be classified as marine ecoregions. A global map of these can be downloaded as a shapefile.

IUCN Red List

The IUCN Red List gives accounts on threatened species. Assigned threat levels range from least concern to extinct. The database can be searched for marine species threatened by invasive species, using filters.

Methods

Data retrieval

The content of each database, complete or only in parts, is retrieved via data scraping. Python is used as the programming language of choice. For each database, Python scripts are created that collect the information automatically. Filters for invasiveness and marine distributions are set. Due to the different set-ups of the various sources, the code varies per database. As soon as the scraping is finalized, the scripts will be posted here.

Data processing

After scraping the data, it has to be put together and matched. The merging of the databases' information is automated by another document. As a starting point serves WoRMS's taxonomy and additional information. If species in one of the other databases are marked as invasive, but not in WoRMS, the marine invasive species list is appended. Details on introduction pathways, impacts, and distributions are allocated. The species are looked up in OBIS via their scientific name in order to get the respective point distribution records.
Hereby, both the coordinate records and the country-/sea area wise occurrences are allocated to marine ecoregions. The aim of this step is to bring the spatial information on native, alien, and total ranges on the same level. Since the occurrences mapped by OBIS show both native and alien ranges combined, the descriptive native ranges of WoRMS, GISD, CABI, and the Nature Conservancy are then to be subtracted in order to get an overview of the alien ranges. Note that it is the native ranges that are subtracted and not the alien ones. This is due to the possibility that the respective species are invading other ranges, too, which might not be reflected in the databases on invasive species yet. It is more secure to choose the native range as the subtrahend.

Data storage

The merged and refined data will be accessible on a MongoDB platform. As interface serves the Studio3T application, available for download here.

Miscellaneous

The information on environmental conditions of species provided by OBIS could be used to predict potential invasive ranges by matching it with oceanographic data, e.g. salinity and temperature.
